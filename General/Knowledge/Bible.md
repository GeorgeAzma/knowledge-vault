### Genesis

God creates the universe in six days — light, sky, land, seas, plants, animals, and finally **Adam**, the first man, formed from dust. God places him in the **Garden of Eden**, a paradise, and creates **Eve** from Adam's rib as his companion. They live in perfection — naked, unashamed, in direct relationship with God. The only rule: don't eat from the **Tree of Knowledge of Good and Evil**.

A serpent — cunning and subtle — approaches Eve and plants doubt. _"Did God really say that? You won't die. You'll become like God."_ Eve eats. Adam eats. Immediately they feel shame, hide from God, and make clothes from fig leaves. God confronts them. Adam blames Eve. Eve blames the serpent. God curses all three — the serpent to crawl on its belly, Eve to suffer in childbirth, Adam to toil the ground. They are **expelled from Eden** forever, and an angel with a flaming sword guards the way back.

---
### Cain & Abel

Adam and Eve have two sons. **Cain** is a farmer. **Abel** is a shepherd. Both offer sacrifices to God. God favors Abel's offering over Cain's — the text doesn't fully explain why, but Cain burns with jealousy. God warns him: _"Sin is crouching at your door."_ Cain ignores it, lures Abel into a field, and kills him — the first murder in human history.

God asks Cain where Abel is. Cain gives history's first sarcastic answer: _"Am I my brother's keeper?"_ God curses Cain to wander the earth as a fugitive. Cain fears others will kill him, so God puts a mark on him for protection. Cain settles east of Eden in the land of **Nod**.

### Noah & The Flood

Generations pass and humanity becomes thoroughly corrupt — violence, wickedness everywhere. God regrets creating humans and decides to wipe the slate clean. But one man stands out: **Noah**, described as righteous and blameless.

God tells Noah to build a massive wooden **ark** and load it with his wife, his three sons (**Shem, Ham, Japheth**) and their wives, plus two of every animal on earth. Noah spends years building while the world mocks him. Then the rains come — **40 days and 40 nights** — floodwaters rise until every mountain is submerged. Everything outside the ark dies.

The waters slowly recede. Noah sends out a **raven**, then a **dove**. The dove returns with an olive branch — land is near. The ark rests on **Mount Ararat**. God makes a covenant with Noah — _never again will he flood the entire earth_ — and places a **rainbow** in the sky as the sign of that promise.

---
### The Tower of Babel

Noah's descendants multiply and all speak one language. They settle in a plain and decide to build a city with a tower reaching to heaven — partly out of ambition, partly to make a name for themselves and avoid being scattered. God sees this and is concerned about what unified humanity might achieve without limits. He **confuses their language** so they can no longer understand each other, and scatters them across the earth. This is the Bible's explanation for the world's many languages and nations.

---
### Abraham — Father of Nations

God calls a man named **Abram** living in **Ur** (modern Iraq) to leave everything — his country, his people, his father's household — and go to a land God would show him. No map. No destination. Just _go_. Abram goes.

God makes a **covenant** with him: _"I will make you into a great nation. I will bless you. All peoples on earth will be blessed through you."_ He and his wife **Sarai** travel to **Canaan**. But there's a problem — Sarai is barren. Years pass. No child. They grow old.

Sarai, impatient, gives her Egyptian servant **Hagar** to Abram as a second wife to produce an heir. Hagar conceives and gives birth to **Ishmael** — who becomes the patriarch of Arab nations in Islamic tradition. But God clarifies that Ishmael is not the child of the covenant.

When Abram is 99, God reaffirms the covenant, renames him **Abraham** (_"father of many nations"_), renames Sarai to **Sarah**, and promises that Sarah herself — at age 90 — will have a son. Abraham laughs. Later, Sarah laughs too when she overhears angels repeat the promise. The son born from this impossible pregnancy is named **Isaac** — meaning _"he laughs."_

Then comes the most devastating test. God tells Abraham to take Isaac — his beloved, miracle, only son — to Mount Moriah and **sacrifice him**. Abraham obeys without recorded argument. He binds Isaac on an altar, raises the knife — and an angel stops him at the last second. A ram caught in a thicket is sacrificed instead. God says: _"Now I know you fear God."_ This event, known as the **Akedah**, is one of the most analyzed moments in all religious literature.

---
### Sodom & Gomorrah

Before Isaac's birth, Abraham encounters three mysterious visitors — widely understood as angels or divine figures — who tell him Sarah will have a son. They also reveal that two cities, **Sodom and Gomorrah**, are so wicked that God plans to destroy them. Abraham boldly **negotiates with God** — _"What if there are 50 righteous people? 40? 30? 10?"_ God agrees each time to spare the cities for the righteous. But not even 10 righteous people are found.

Abraham's nephew **Lot** lives in Sodom. The angels go to warn him. The men of Sodom surround Lot's house demanding he hand over the visitors. Lot refuses. The angels blind the mob and urge Lot to flee immediately with his family. As they escape, fire and sulfur rain down destroying both cities. Lot's wife, told not to look back, **looks back and turns into a pillar of salt**.

---
### Isaac & Rebekah

Isaac grows up, and Abraham sends a servant back to his homeland to find Isaac a wife from his own people rather than the Canaanites. The servant prays for a sign at a well — that the right woman would offer water to both him and his camels. **Rebekah** does exactly that. She agrees to leave her family and travel to Canaan to marry a man she's never met. Isaac sees her coming across a field, and the text says he loved her. She comforts him after his mother Sarah's death.

---
### Jacob — The Wrestler

Isaac and Rebekah have **twin sons** — **Esau**, born first, hairy and ruddy, a hunter; and **Jacob**, born grabbing Esau's heel, smooth-skinned and cunning. The birthright and blessing — inheritance of the covenant — belongs to the firstborn Esau.

One day Esau comes home famished from hunting. Jacob is cooking stew. Esau sells his birthright for a bowl of lentils. Later, when aging and nearly blind Isaac is ready to give his final blessing to Esau, Rebekah and Jacob scheme. Jacob disguises himself with goatskin on his arms to feel hairy like Esau, and tricks his father into blessing him instead. When Esau discovers this he is furious enough to kill Jacob. Jacob flees.

On the run, Jacob sleeps under the stars and has a famous dream — a **ladder reaching to heaven** with angels ascending and descending. God speaks to him and reaffirms the covenant promises. Jacob wakes and names the place **Bethel** (_"house of God"_).

He travels to his uncle **Laban's** household and falls deeply in love with Laban's younger daughter **Rachel**. He works seven years to earn her as his wife. On the wedding night, Laban substitutes the older daughter **Leah** under the veil. Jacob wakes up to the wrong wife. He works another seven years for Rachel. He ends up with both sisters as wives, plus their two servants **Zilpah** and **Bilhah** — twelve sons and a daughter altogether, between the four women.

After twenty years, Jacob leaves Laban's household and heads home — terrified of facing Esau. The night before the reunion, a mysterious figure wrestles with him until dawn. Jacob refuses to let go until the figure blesses him. The figure dislocates Jacob's hip and renames him **Israel** — _"one who struggles with God."_ The next day, Esau embraces him in tears. The brothers reconcile.
